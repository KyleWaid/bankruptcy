## 1. DEATH OF NON-FILING SPOUSE DURING CHAPTER 13

### Since wife was NOT on the bankruptcy:
- Her death does not trigger automatic dismissal or conversion
- BUT it changes the financial picture significantly:
  - Loss of her income (if any was included on Schedule I)
  - Potential new income: SS survivor benefits, life insurance
  - Household size may change for means test purposes

### Required action: File motion to modify plan (11 USC §1329)
- Reflect new income picture on amended Schedules I and J
- Propose reduced payments based on changed circumstances

### If modification isn't feasible, alternatives:
1. **Hardship discharge** (§1328(b)) — death of spouse is textbook qualifying circumstance
   - Must show: (a) not debtor's fault, (b) creditors got at least Ch.7 amount, (c) modification not practicable
   - NOTE: Hardship discharge is narrower — §523(a) debts remain nondischargeable
2. **Convert to Chapter 7** (§1307(a)) — absolute right
3. **Dismiss** (§1307(b)) — near-absolute right

### Means test implications after spouse's death:
- Household size may decrease (was spouse counted?)
- Deceased spouse's income drops out entirely
- Debtor may move from above-median to below-median → potentially 3-year plan instead of 5
- Should file amended Form 22C

## 2. SOCIAL SECURITY SURVIVOR BENEFITS FOR CHILDREN

### Core protection — TRIPLE-LAYERED:

**Layer 1: Federal statute (42 USC §407(a))**
- SS benefits NOT subject to execution, levy, attachment, garnishment, or other legal process
- Absolute federal protection

**Layer 2: Bankruptcy Code (11 USC §101(10A))**
- SS benefits explicitly EXCLUDED from "current monthly income"
- Therefore excluded from means test AND disposable income calculations

**Layer 3: Representative payee doctrine**
- Parent receives SS survivor benefits as representative payee for minor children
- Funds legally belong to the CHILDREN, not the debtor-parent
- Not debtor's property → not property of estate under §541
- Parent has fiduciary duty to use for children's needs

### The trustee CANNOT capture children's SS survivor benefits.

### Key case law:
- *In re Ragos* (Bankr. D. Minn.) — SS benefits cannot be included in disposable income
- *In re Scholz*, 437 B.R. 860 (Bankr. E.D. Wis. 2010) — SS benefits for dependents excluded from means test
- Eighth Circuit (covers MN): SS income cannot be involuntarily committed to plan

### ⚠️ CRITICAL PRACTICAL POINT:
- **Keep SS survivor benefits in a SEPARATE dedicated bank account**
- Do NOT commingle with other funds
- If commingled, tracing disputes can arise
- Still list on Schedule I for disclosure, but note they are excluded from disposable income

### Caution:
- Some trustees may argue SS income should be considered under "good faith" (§1325(a)(3))
- These arguments have generally failed but debtor should be prepared

## 3. LIFE INSURANCE PROCEEDS

### Minnesota exemptions — TWO key statutes:

**Minn. Stat. §550.39:**
- Proceeds payable to surviving spouse or children of the deceased are exempt from creditors of the deceased insured
- Potentially unlimited for this category

**Minn. Stat. §61A.12:**
- Proceeds exempt from creditors of the BENEFICIARY up to a dollar limit
- Historically ~$46,000-$50,000 (VERIFY CURRENT AMOUNT)
- This is the figure that typically matters in bankruptcy

### Chapter 13 complications (11 USC §1306(a)):
- Life insurance proceeds received during Ch.13 ARE property of the estate
- BUT exemptions still apply — exempt portion is protected
- Any NON-EXEMPT portion may be subject to trustee's motion to modify plan

### Debtor's obligations:
- **MUST disclose receipt** of life insurance proceeds (11 USC §521)
- Failure to disclose → sanctions, denial of discharge, or dismissal
- Should immediately claim exemption on amended schedules

### Strategic considerations:
- Claim all available exemptions immediately
- If proceeds exceed exempt amount, trustee may move to modify plan to increase unsecured creditor payments
- Timing matters: exempt proceeds spent on reasonable living expenses before trustee acts may no longer be available to capture

## 4. 401(k) DURING CHAPTER 13

### 401(k) Account Protection:
- **Fully exempt** under ERISA (29 USC §1056(d)), federal bankruptcy law (11 USC §522), and Minnesota law (Minn. Stat. §550.37 subd. 24)
- The account itself is NOT property of the estate (*Patterson v. Shumate*, 504 U.S. 753)
- Trustee cannot touch money IN the 401(k)

### 401(k) LOANS during Chapter 13:
- **Generally require court approval**
- Considered new debt during the plan
- Loan repayments reduce take-home pay → reduce disposable income for plan
- Trustee routinely objects to unauthorized post-petition 401(k) loans
- District of Minnesota likely requires court permission per local rules/trustee guidelines

### 401(k) HARDSHIP WITHDRAWALS during Chapter 13:
- Nothing in Bankruptcy Code explicitly prohibits
- BUT once withdrawn, funds MAY LOSE exempt status
- *In re Mati* (Bankr. D. Minn.) — withdrawn retirement funds may no longer be exempt
- Withdrawal proceeds become property of estate under §1306(a)
- Trustee can argue non-exempt withdrawn funds should go to plan

### Tax consequences of hardship withdrawal:
- Federal income tax at marginal rate
- Minnesota state tax (5.35% - 9.85%)
- 10% federal early withdrawal penalty if under 59½
- Creates new tax liability — potentially a new debt problem

### Voluntary 401(k) contributions during Chapter 13:
- Controversial — some courts allow reasonable contributions (5-10%)
- Others view as reducing disposable income at creditors' expense
- *In re Seafort*, 669 F.3d 662 (6th Cir. 2012) — voluntary contributions not reasonably necessary
- Eighth Circuit hasn't directly ruled but trustee likely to resist large contributions

## 5. PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER — THE SCENARIO

For a MN Chapter 13 debtor whose non-filing spouse dies:

1. **Immediate crisis**: Loss of spouse's income makes current plan infeasible → file to modify
2. **SS survivor benefits**: EXCLUDED from disposable income — trustee cannot capture — keep in separate account
3. **Life insurance**: Exempt under MN law (subject to dollar limits under §61A.12) — must disclose — claim exemption immediately
4. **401(k)**: Do NOT withdraw if avoidable — withdrawn funds may lose protection and create tax liability

### Best strategic path (research observation):
- (a) File to modify plan immediately reflecting income loss
- (b) Claim all exemptions on life insurance proceeds
- (c) Rely on SSA exclusion for children's survivor benefits
- (d) Avoid 401(k) withdrawals if at all possible
- (e) If modification not practicable → hardship discharge under §1328(b)

## 1. HARDSHIP DISCHARGE — DEATH OF SPOUSE

### In re Bandilli, 231 B.R. 836 (B.A.P. 1st Cir. 1999)
- **Holding:** No "catastrophic" event required for hardship discharge under §1328(b)
- Court refused to read the word "catastrophic" into the statute
- Death of a spouse is widely cited as a textbook qualifying circumstance under prong (1)
- Debtor must still satisfy all three prongs:
  1. Failure not debtor's fault ✓ (death of spouse)
  2. Unsecured creditors got at least Ch.7 amount
  3. Modification not practicable
- Hardship discharge is narrower — §523(a) exceptions survive

## 2. SOCIAL SECURITY SURVIVOR BENEFITS — PROTECTED

### Baud v. Carroll, 634 F.3d 327 (6th Cir. 2011)
- **Holding:** Social Security benefits are EXCLUDED from projected disposable income in Chapter 13
- Congress intended departure from pre-BAPCPA practice
- SS benefits cannot be forced into the plan

### In re Canniff, 498 B.R. 213 (Bankr. S.D. Ind. 2013)
- **Holding:** Debtor's decision NOT to contribute SS income to plan is NOT bad faith
- Trustee argued excluding $700/month SS payment was bad faith — court disagreed
- Statutorily excluded income cannot be treated as bad faith to withhold

### Bottom line: Majority of courts reject trustee attempts to capture SS benefits through "good faith" arguments under §1325(a)(3). Debtor may voluntarily include them but CANNOT be compelled.

## 3. 401(k) LOANS & WITHDRAWALS DURING CHAPTER 13

### Pre-petition 401(k) Loan Repayments
- **11 USC §1322(f):** Plan may not materially alter terms of a 401(k) loan
- Loan repayments EXCLUDED from disposable income under §1325
- Pre-petition loans can generally continue

### NEW Loans/Withdrawals During Plan
- Generally requires court approval (§1305 — new debt prohibition)
- Courts rarely grant; discretionary income should go to plan payments
- Trustees have successfully objected → consequences include dismissal or trustee capturing funds

### Davis v. Helbling (In re Davis), 960 F.3d 346 (6th Cir. 2020)
- Pre-petition voluntary 401(k) contributions may continue and are excluded from disposable income
- Must be continuation of pre-petition contributions, not new/restarted

### ⚠️ CIRCUIT SPLIT: Eighth Circuit has NOT issued published opinion on 401(k) contributions and disposable income. Significant divergence between circuits. 11 USC §541(b)(7)(A) is the statutory battleground.

## 4. LIFE INSURANCE PROCEEDS — THE DANGER ZONE

### In re Roscoe, Case No. 13-6517 (Bankr. M.D. Fla. 2017)
- Wife died during Chapter 13 plan (more than 180 days after filing)
- Husband claimed proceeds weren't estate property under §541(a)(5) 180-day rule
- **Court held: §1306(a) EXPANDS the estate beyond §541(a)(5)**
- Life insurance proceeds belong to the Chapter 13 estate REGARDLESS of 180-day limit
- **Proceeds ordered turned over to trustee**

### Security Bank v. Neiman, 1 F.3d 687 (8th Cir. 1993)
- **Eighth Circuit follows "Estate Preservation" approach**
- Chapter 13 estate continues after confirmation
- Includes ALL after-acquired property under §1306(a)
- **THIS IS THE CONTROLLING CIRCUIT FOR MINNESOTA**
- Eighth Circuit would likely follow Roscoe result

### BUT: Minnesota exemptions still apply
- Even though proceeds are estate property, the EXEMPT portion is protected
- Minn. Stat. §550.37 subd. 10 + §64B.18: **$54,000 + $13,500 per dependent**
- With 3 kids: **$94,500 exempt**
- Anything ABOVE $94,500 → trustee can claim for creditors

### ⚠️ CRITICAL: You MUST disclose receipt of life insurance proceeds. You MUST claim exemption on amended schedules. Failure to disclose = sanctions/dismissal/denial of discharge.

## 5. PLAN MODIFICATION AFTER DEATH OF HOUSEHOLD MEMBER

### Statutory basis: 11 USC §1329
- Debtor, trustee, or unsecured creditor can seek modification anytime before payment completion
- Can change: payment amounts, plan length, creditor distributions

### Circuit split on "change of circumstances" requirement:
- **Eleventh Circuit** (*Whaley v. Guillen*, 2020): §1329 does NOT impose change-of-circumstances requirement
- **Eighth Circuit**: No definitive published ruling, but courts generally apply good faith + discretionary analysis
- **Death of household member universally recognized** as qualifying change where courts do require it

### No published MN opinion found on this specific issue
- Likely resolved by stipulation or unpublished order in most cases
- PACER would be the source for unpublished orders

## 1. EIGHTH CIRCUIT GOOD FAITH TEST — FAVORABLE TO YOUR FACTS

### In re Estus, 695 F.2d 311 (8th Cir. 1982) — THE controlling test

The Eighth Circuit uses a "totality of circumstances" test with these factors:

1. Amount of proposed payments and debtor's surplus
2. Employment history, ability to earn, likelihood of future increases
3. Probable duration of the plan
4. Accuracy of plan statements (and whether inaccuracies mislead)
5. Preferential treatment between classes
6. Extent secured claims are modified
7. Type of debt and whether nondischargeable in Ch.7
8. **Existence of special circumstances such as inordinate medical expenses**
9. Frequency of prior bankruptcy filings
10. **Motivation and sincerity of debtor in seeking Ch.13 relief**

### In re LeMaire, 898 F.2d 1346 (8th Cir. 1990)
- Reaffirmed Estus factors
- No single factor is dispositive
- Weight of each factor varies with circumstances

### Application to Kyle's Case:
- **Factor 8 (special circumstances):** Wife's alcoholism, suicide, single parenthood — strongly favorable
- **Factor 10 (motivation/sincerity):** Catching up on payments, withdrawing from 401(k) to pay creditors, communicating with attorney — strongly favorable
- Payment irregularity (negative in isolation) must be viewed alongside extraordinary personal circumstances
- **The Eighth Circuit explicitly requires totality analysis — no single factor controls**

## 2. SPOUSE SUICIDE DURING CHAPTER 13

No published opinion found specifically addressing spouse suicide during Ch.13. However:

### In re Hoover (Bankr. N.D. Cal.)
- Granted hardship discharge after debtor died with one payment remaining
- Death is "understandably held to be a circumstance beyond the debtor's control" under 1328(b)
- Principle applies to spouse's death: unanticipated death is the paradigmatic qualifying event

### In re Graham, 63 B.R. 95 (Bankr. E.D. Pa.)
- Foundational case for administering Ch.13 when debtor dies before completing payments

### Key Legal Point:
- Manner of death (suicide) does NOT matter for 1328(b) analysis
- The statute asks: was failure to complete payments due to "circumstances for which the debtor should not justly be held accountable"
- Spouse's suicide is precisely such a circumstance — beyond surviving debtor's control
- Exception only if surviving spouse CAUSED the death — suicide plainly does not qualify

## 3. HARDSHIP DISCHARGE — THREE-PART TEST

### In re Bandilli, 231 B.R. 836 (BAP 1st Cir. 1999)
1. Failure to complete payments due to circumstances debtor should not justly be held accountable for ✓ (spouse death/suicide)
2. Unsecured creditors received at least Ch.7 liquidation amount ✓ (need to verify)
3. Modification of plan is not practicable (depends on expense analysis)

### In re Edwards, 207 B.R. 728 (Bankr. N.D. Fla. 1997)
Courts should examine:
1. Was there a change of circumstance debtor was not accountable for? ✓
2. Was the change substantial? ✓ (spouse death + becoming single parent of 3)
3. Was it not foreseeable at confirmation? ✓
4. Did debtor make every effort to overcome? ✓ (401(k) withdrawals, catch-up payments)

## 4. ALCOHOLISM AS "SPECIAL CIRCUMSTANCE"

- Alcoholism recognized as medical condition (AMA classification)
- Courts permit deductions for addiction-related expenses in means test
- Non-filing spouse's alcoholism creating household financial chaos is a sympathetic and understandable explanation for financial strain
- Courts evaluating good faith don't require debtor to explain away every difficulty — they look at overall honesty and effort

## 6. "BEHIND-CATCH UP-BEHIND-CATCH UP" PATTERN

### Courts Generally View This as GOOD FAITH:
- Demonstrates commitment to plan and genuine effort to comply
- Bad-faith debtor simply stops paying
- Courts distinguish between chronic inability (modify the plan) vs. strategic non-compliance (bad faith)
- Kyle's pattern + extraordinary circumstances = chronic inability, NOT bad faith

### Partial Payments = Good Faith:
- Partial payments demonstrate good faith effort
- Judges view partial payments more favorably than no payment at all

### Key Factors Courts Consider:
1. Is debtor communicating with trustee and attorney? ✓ (extensively documented in emails)
2. Is there a legitimate reason for irregularity? ✓ (alcoholic spouse, death, single parent)
3. Is debtor making genuine efforts to comply? ✓ (401(k) withdrawals, bulk catch-up payments)
4. Would creditors receive more through continuation than dismissal? ✓ (likely yes)

## 7. 401(K) WITHDRAWALS — DOUBLE-EDGED SWORD

**Positive framing:** Powerfully demonstrates good faith — debtor literally sacrificing retirement to honor obligations to creditors

**Negative risk:** Courts view withdrawals with skepticism because:
- Withdrawn funds may be treated as disposable income
- Some courts consider it unnecessary self-harm (401(k) is protected in bankruptcy)
- Attorney should frame as evidence of extraordinary good faith, NOT a sustainable funding source

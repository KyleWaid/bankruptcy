# Chapter 13 Outcomes, Failure Patterns & Lessons Learned

**DISCLAIMER: This is legal research/information, not legal advice.**

---

## 1. NATIONAL CHAPTER 13 COMPLETION RATES

**The reality is harsh:**

| Year | Discharge Rate | Dismissed/Converted |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| 2024 | **49%** | 51% |
| 2023 | 52% | 48% |
| 2019 | 43% | 57% |
| Historical avg (2010-2016) | **33-39%** | 61-67% |

- Of 194,055 Chapter 13 consumer cases closed in 2024, ~97,063 resulted in discharge
- Variance by district is enormous: 76% (E.D. Washington) down to below 30% in some districts
- **More than half of all Chapter 13 cases fail**

Sources: 2024 BAPCPA Report (uscourts.gov), Debt.org, DismissalRate.org

## 2. TOP REASONS CHAPTER 13 CASES FAIL (Ranked)

1. **Failure to make plan payments** — 51% of all dismissals cite nonpayment. Job loss and income disruption are leading triggers.
2. **Failure to file required documents** — Missing schedules, tax returns, supporting docs.
3. **Failure to attend mandatory meetings/complete courses** — Missing 341 meeting or debtor education course. Tragic: completing years of payments but forgetting the education course = automatic denial of discharge.
4. **Falling behind on post-petition obligations** — Mortgage, property taxes, insurance during plan period.
5. **Incurring new unauthorized debt** — Cars, credit cards, 401(k) loans without court approval.
6. **Life disruptions** — Medical emergencies, divorce, death of spouse, family crises.
7. **Unrealistic plans** — Every dollar committed, zero buffer for emergencies.

## 3. WHAT SUCCESSFUL CHAPTER 13 COMPLETERS DO DIFFERENTLY

### #1: They have an attorney
- Pro se completion rate: **2.3%**
- With attorney: **~33%+**
- Legal representation is the single largest predictor of success

### #2: They use payroll deduction (wage orders)
- Payments taken from paycheck before debtor sees money
- Dramatically higher completion rates vs. voluntary monthly payments
- Removes the decision point and temptation to divert funds

### #3: They budget realistically with buffer room
- Modest allowances for vehicle maintenance, medical expenses, household repairs
- NOT committing every dollar of disposable income

### #4: They modify plans immediately when circumstances change
- Contact attorney BEFORE missing a payment, not after
- File modification promptly — don't wait for trustee's motion to dismiss

### #5: They maintain stable employment/income

### #6: They file clean, complete, accurate initial petitions
- Avoids trustee objections and delays

## 4. HIGH-INCOME EARNERS IN CHAPTER 13

### Structural disadvantages:
- **Mandatory 5-year plans** (above median) — more time for things to go wrong
- **Higher payment obligations** — all projected disposable income for 60 months
- **More scrutiny** — courts challenge 401(k) contributions, lifestyle expenses, private school, etc.

### Philadelphia Federal Reserve Working Paper 14-33 findings:
- Negative income shocks early in the plan significantly raise dismissal probability
- Longer plans = more exposure to disruption
- The 5-year mandate may actually be counterproductive (doesn't improve creditor recovery)
- **The first 12-18 months are the highest-risk period**

### Below-median advantage (relevant to your case at $145k vs $146k median):
- 3-year plan possible — less exposure time
- Actual expenses used (not IRS standards) — more flexibility
- Less court scrutiny on lifestyle expenses

## 5. DEATH OF SPOUSE DURING CHAPTER 13 — OUTCOMES

### When deceased was primary/sole earner:
- **Hardship discharge** is the most common successful outcome
- §1328(b) — death is textbook "circumstances beyond debtor's control"

### When income loss is partial:
- **Plan modification** is preferred path
- Reduce payments to reflect new reality

### Key points:
- Case does NOT automatically dismiss
- Courts evaluate feasibility and best interests
- FRBP 1016 governs — court can allow case to continue if further administration is possible
- Non-debtor party completing deceased debtor's obligations cannot amend the plan

## 6. 401(k) BORROWING — IMPACT ON CASES

### Court approval required for new loans during Ch.13
- Failure to get approval = compliance violation → potential dismissal

### Competing court frameworks on 401(k) contributions:
| Framework | Case | Rule |
|-----------|------|------|
| Johnson View (majority) | *Baxter v. Johnson*, 346 B.R. 256 | Contributions allowed if "good faith" — considers age, health, balance, history |
| Prigge View | *In re Prigge*, 441 B.R. 667 | Never permitted — all disposable income to creditors |
| Seafort View | — | Only at pre-filing contribution level |

### 401(k) loan risks during Ch.13:
- Loan repayments reduce disposable income → less for creditors
- Courts may deny if unsecured creditors receiving nothing
- If loan defaults during plan → taxable distribution + 10% penalty → new financial crisis
- Pattern of unauthorized borrowing viewed as bad faith

### Practitioner consensus:
401(k) raiding is a red flag constellation: unauthorized borrowing + reduced income + tax penalties + court perception of bad faith → high risk of case failure

## 7. MOST COMMON MISTAKES LEADING TO DISMISSAL

1. **Missing plan payments** — 60-70% of dismissals. First missed payment triggers cascade.
2. **Not completing debtor education course** — Automatic denial of discharge even after years of payments.
3. **Not disclosing financial changes** — Job loss, raises, inheritance, tax refunds, new household members.
4. **Incurring new debt without court approval** — Cars, credit cards, 401(k) loans.
5. **Missing 341 meeting** — Or failing to provide required documents at meeting.
6. **Inaccurate/incomplete schedules** — Omitting assets, understating income.
7. **Letting insurance lapse** — Homeowner's or auto insurance during plan.
8. **Not filing post-petition tax returns** — Required annually, must provide to trustee.
9. **Falling behind on domestic support** — Current child support/alimony = prerequisite for eligibility.

## 9. SINGLE PARENTS IN CHAPTER 13 — SPECIFIC CHALLENGES

### Unique vulnerabilities:
- **Single income, no backup** — No second earner to absorb shocks
- **Child support is non-dischargeable priority** — Must be paid in full through plan AND stay current post-petition
- **Childcare costs are volatile** — Provider changes, schedule shifts, sick kids
- **Time poverty** — Paperwork, meetings, attorney communication on top of solo parenting
- **No emergency reserves** — Any unexpected expense can derail the plan

### Best practices for single parents:
1. **Build court-disclosed emergency fund** — Even $50-100/month, fully disclosed
2. **Use payroll deduction** — Removes manual payment burden
3. **Budget conservatively from start** — Realistic childcare, transport, medical estimates
4. **Communicate immediately when circumstances change** — Modify BEFORE missing payments
5. **Maintain domestic support compliance** — Current payments are prerequisite
6. **Line up ongoing financial counseling** — Not just legal support

## 10. LESSONS FROM FAILED CASES — WHAT THEY'D DO DIFFERENTLY

### "I would have been honest about my budget from day one."
Plans built on optimistic projections or artificially low expenses collapse. The plan was never realistic.

### "I would have let go of the house sooner."
**Major pattern:** Filing Ch.13 to save a home that was already unaffordable. Pouring 3-5 years of payments into a plan only to lose the house anyway. Practitioners call this one of the most common and devastating mistakes.

### "I would have used payroll deduction."
Manual payments were too easy to divert when something came up. Wage orders remove that decision point.

### "I would have called my attorney the moment things changed."
Many dismissed cases involve 2-3 missed payments before contacting the lawyer. By then, the trustee arrearage is too large to cure.

### "I would have explored Chapter 7 first."
Many Ch.13 filers could have qualified for Ch.7 and received discharge in months, not years.

### "I would not have raided my retirement."
Borrowing from 401(k) before filing creates complications; borrowing during the plan without approval is a compliance violation.

### "I would have built an emergency fund into the plan."
Lack of any financial cushion is one of the most cited but least discussed reasons for failure.

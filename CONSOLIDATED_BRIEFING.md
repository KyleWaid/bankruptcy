# SECTION 4: SOCIAL SECURITY SURVIVOR BENEFITS

## THE BOTTOM LINE: The trustee CANNOT touch these.

### Triple-layered protection:

**1. Federal law (42 USC §407(a)):** SS benefits are not subject to execution, levy, attachment, garnishment, or other legal process.

**2. Bankruptcy Code (11 USC §101(10A)):** SS benefits are explicitly EXCLUDED from "current monthly income" — excluded from means test AND disposable income calculations.

**3. Representative payee doctrine:** You receive these benefits on behalf of your minor children. The money legally belongs to them, not you. Not your property = not estate property under §541.

### Eighth Circuit (covers Minnesota):
- SS income cannot be involuntarily committed to the plan
- *In re Ragos* (Bankr. D. Minn.) — confirmed SS excluded from disposable income

### ⚠️ CRITICAL ACTION ITEM:
**Open a SEPARATE bank account for SS survivor benefits. Do NOT commingle with other funds.** If SS money is mixed with other money in one account, it creates tracing disputes. Keep it clean.

### Have you applied yet?
- Social Security survivor benefits for dependent children of a deceased parent
- Each child may be eligible for up to 75% of the deceased parent's benefit amount
- Family maximum applies (typically 150-180% of deceased's benefit)
- Apply at SSA.gov or your local Social Security office
- Benefits can be retroactive to date of death (up to 6 months back in some cases)

# SECTION 6: THE 401(k) PROBLEM

## Your 401(k) is fully protected IN the account.
- ERISA, federal bankruptcy law, and Minnesota law all protect it
- Trustee cannot touch money sitting in your 401(k)

## BUT: You've been borrowing from it. Here's the problem:

### 401(k) Loans during Chapter 13:
- **Generally require court approval** — it's considered new debt
- Loan repayments reduce your take-home pay → reduce disposable income for plan
- If you took loans WITHOUT court approval, the trustee could object
- Your attorney should know about every loan you've taken

### 401(k) Hardship Withdrawals — WORSE:
- Once withdrawn, funds **may lose exempt status**
- Cash in your bank account is NOT protected like cash in your 401(k)
- Trustee can argue withdrawal proceeds should go to plan
- *In re Mati* (Bankr. D. Minn.) — withdrawn retirement funds may lose exemption
- PLUS: You owe income tax + 10% penalty (if under 59½) on every withdrawal

### The math on hardship withdrawals:
- You withdraw $10,000
- Federal tax (~22-24%): -$2,200 to -$2,400
- MN state tax (~7%): -$700
- 10% penalty: -$1,000
- **Net to you: ~$5,900-$6,100**
- You destroyed $10,000 of retirement savings to get ~$6,000
- AND the $6,000 may not even be protected from the trustee

### ⚠️ STOP taking 401(k) withdrawals if at all possible.
This is one of the most destructive financial moves you can make during Chapter 13. Every dollar you pull out costs you roughly 40 cents in taxes/penalties AND may be claimable by the trustee.

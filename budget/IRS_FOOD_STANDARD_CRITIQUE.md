# IRS National Standard for Food — Why $1,043/mo for a Family of 4 Is Not Realistic in 2026

**Compiled: April 15, 2026**
**For: Case 24-32819 — Plan Modification Record**
**Subject: Justification for grocery line item exceeding IRS National Standard**

---

## THE STANDARD AT ISSUE

The IRS Collection Financial Standards (used by Chapter 13 trustees as a baseline of reasonable expense) currently allow:

- **Food: $1,043/month for a family of 4**
- This works out to approximately **$8.69 per person per day** — covering all meals, snacks, beverages, and food-related supplies.

This figure has barely moved in years despite a **~25% increase in food-at-home CPI from 2021 through 2025** (Bureau of Labor Statistics, Consumer Price Index for Food at Home, series CUUR0000SAF11). The standard is administratively frozen; grocery prices are not.

---

## COMPARISON TO THE FEDERAL GOVERNMENT'S OWN FOOD COST DATA

The U.S. Department of Agriculture publishes monthly Food Plan cost estimates at four price tiers. For a household composition matching the debtor's (one adult + three children/teens, ages 11, 13, 15), recent USDA estimates are approximately:

| USDA Food Plan tier | Approx. monthly cost (family of 4 with teens) |
|---|---|
| Thrifty (the basis for SNAP benefits) | $1,250 – $1,350 |
| Low-Cost | $1,650 – $1,750 |
| Moderate-Cost | $2,050 – $2,150 |
| Liberal | $2,500+ |

**The IRS National Standard of $1,043 falls below even the USDA Thrifty Plan**, which itself is the calculated minimum cost of a nutritionally adequate diet using the cheapest available foods, designed for SNAP allotment purposes.

In other words: the IRS standard, applied to a household with three teenagers, would require the debtor to feed his family for **less than the federal government's own minimum-adequate food budget**.

## REGIONAL COST GAP — FOOD IS A LOCAL EXPENSE, NOT A NATIONAL ONE

The IRS Food Standard is a **single national figure**. It does not vary by state, metro area, or zip code. This is a fundamental flaw when applied to households in higher-cost regions.

**The debtor lives in the Twin Cities metropolitan area (Eagan, Dakota County, MN).** Food prices in this region are documented as materially above the national average:

- The Bureau of Labor Statistics' Consumer Expenditure Survey and Regional Price Parities (RPP) data consistently show the Minneapolis-St. Paul metro running above the national mean for food-at-home costs.
- The Council for Community and Economic Research (C2ER) Cost of Living Index regularly places Minneapolis-St. Paul's grocery index above 100 (national baseline), often in the 102-108 range depending on the survey period.
- Major Twin Cities grocery chains (Cub Foods, Lunds & Byerlys, Kowalski's, Whole Foods) and the regional dominance of higher-priced retailers reflect a market where the cheapest national tier (Aldi, Walmart) is not as price-competitive on the same basket as in lower-cost regions.
- USDA's regional cost adjustments and SNAP allotment variations between low-cost and high-cost states confirm that food costs vary by 10-20%+ across U.S. regions.

**By contrast**, the IRS Food Standard:
- Does not adjust for regional cost-of-living
- Does not account for metro vs. rural cost gaps
- Uses a single number applied identically in Mississippi (lowest food costs nationally) and Hawaii (among the highest)
- Has no built-in regional escalator

This means a $1,043/month food budget that might be tight-but-workable in a low-cost state becomes meaningfully below realistic floor in a higher-cost metro like the Twin Cities.

The IRS *Local* Standards (which the IRS does maintain for housing and transportation) prove the principle: where the IRS itself recognizes regional cost variation, it adjusts. The fact that the Food Standard alone is left as a national flat number is an administrative oversight, not a considered policy that food costs the same everywhere.

## LEGAL FRAMING

The IRS Local and National Standards are **a floor of automatic reasonableness, not a cap**. Bankruptcy courts have repeatedly held that documented actual expenses control over the standards when the actual expenses are reasonable for the household.

- The Local Standards represent "the maximum allowance the IRS will accept *without further inquiry*" — they are a safe-harbor, not a ceiling. (See IRS Internal Revenue Manual 5.15.1.)
- A debtor's actual reasonable and necessary expenses control under 11 U.S.C. §1325(b)(2) for a debtor with above-median income, and even more flexibility applies for below-median debtors.
- See generally *In re Welsh*, 711 F.3d 1120 (9th Cir. 2013); *In re Lara*, 2010 WL 3782060 (Bankr. D. Kan.); *Hamilton v. Lanning*, 560 U.S. 505 (2010) (forward-looking reasonable expense analysis).

The debtor's $1,800 grocery line is documented, supported by USDA cost data, and consistent with realistic feeding costs for the household composition. It should not be reduced to a level that falls below the federal government's own minimum-adequate food budget.
